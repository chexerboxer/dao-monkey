Chapter 1 Pear Sea City Religious Culture Exchange Festival (Part 1)

It all started with this morning's news.

Luo Binhan usually didn't watch TV news. He only turned on the TV for movies or on-demand programs. But that was before. In the past three months, he'd been feeling rather listless, so he rarely watched TV.

But this morning he was preparing to catch a flight—his lawyer mother and his half-sister were currently living abroad, and his mother once again needed him to keep an eye on that troublesome sister—so he got up unusually early. While drowsily frying eggs, he casually turned on the TV, letting the news broadcast play on its own.

"...Last night, a jewelry store in the city's Lake Poplar District was robbed by multiple suspects. Police arrived quickly, and the perpetrators took two hostages with knives, one a female clerk and one a male customer. During the standoff, several suspects accidentally injured their leg arteries with crystal decorations in the store and are now being rushed to the hospital..."

"...The religious culture exchange event starting today... The vice president gave a unique and lively speech on the history of the city's religious architecture..."

"...This footage was captured by a citizen yesterday. A clear meteor can be seen streaking across the clear daytime sky. Experts have proposed three possible hypotheses for this phenomenon..."

Luo Binhan yawned, took his fried eggs to the living room to eat, then grabbed his luggage and left. After getting into a taxi, he remembered he should call his old friend Zhou Yu, so he fished his phone out of his pocket.

For Luo Binhan, few people could compare to Zhou Yu in importance. Their friendship began in elementary school, and they were classmates all the way through high school. Even now, they maintained very close contact. Every time he went abroad, he would report to this good friend beforehand.

The phone rang for a few seconds before it was answered—unlike him, who lived a lazy life, Zhou Yu had long kept the habit of waking up early.

"Hey Zhou Yu. What's up?" Luo Binhan asked listlessly.

On the other end, Zhou Yu hummed in acknowledgment and replied succinctly, "Reading."

He said he was reading, but Luo Binhan knew he probably meant he was working on his thesis. A year ago, Zhou Yu's childhood friend suddenly disappeared. To find her, Zhou Yu had to interrupt his studies and was hospitalized multiple times. Only three months ago, deeply traumatized, he barely recovered, and now through his father's connections, he was looking for a new advisor to pursue his degree.

Luo Binhan explained over the phone that he had to go abroad for a few days, maybe extending to two weeks, and asked him to take care of the parrot at home. Zhou Yu agreed as usual.

The conversation should have ended there, but Luo Binhan was still feeling a bit bored and wanted to chat more with his friend. So he said, "Did you see this morning's news? There was a jewelry robbery near where you live."

Zhou Yu hummed.

"And they took hostages... pretty bold, huh?"

Zhou Yu hummed again.

His friend's response wasn't enthusiastic, but Luo Binhan wasn't deterred. He had an inexplicable, innate passion for dangerous things.

Feeling his freshly shaved chin, he mused, "Don't you think it's strange? Several suspects got their thighs cut by broken crystal jewelry—I can understand feet, but how did they get their thighs hit? Do they all like to ram their legs into broken stuff?"

"Coincidence," Zhou Yu replied on the phone.

"Hey, you're way too calm... So what were you doing last night? Hear any sirens?"

"Mm, I was at the shop picking out your birthday gift."

Luo Binhan was reminded. His birthday was next month, but there wasn't much to look forward to. Ever since his parents divorced, his birthdays had been dull. He was curious about what Zhou Yu planned to buy him, but Zhou Yu's lips were always sealed tight.

Just as he was considering how to pry it out of the guy, the taxi turned into an old street. Luo Binhan's eyes lit up, and he immediately forgot about the birthday gift. He hurriedly said a few words and hung up.

The Ciji Cathedral at the end of the old street was said to have a hundred years of history. In a sense, that wasn't a glorious thing, but undeniably the building was beautiful. It had towering, steep spires and lavish rose windows that attracted many tourists for photos, though the latter were actually installed during renovations in the last decade.

At this moment, the cathedral entrance was piled with fresh bouquets of roses, and two large clusters of colorful balloons were tied to the main door. The most stunning was the five-meter-long red banner that read: "Warmly welcome the venerable monks of Qinglian Temple for exchange." Beneath the banner, in front of the cathedral, stood a line of gray-robed monks.

Luo Binhan was captivated by this surreal scene. Even the taxi driver was mesmerized. He unconsciously slowed down, trying to steal a few more seconds of this mysterious scene.

"Mister, what do you think monks and nuns can talk about?" Luo Binhan asked, eyes fixed.

The taxi driver was insightful. Wiping the steering wheel, he said decisively, "You'd have to ask what attitude their top brass has."

"So can the Buddha and God get along?"

"Depends on the circumstances. We're all under the red flag; who can leave whom? Just make do and get along."

Luo Binhan suddenly felt the driver's words were profound. He developed a deep respect and asked seriously, "Do you believe in Buddhism?"

The driver chuckled. "I follow the Dao."

Luo Binhan realized his mistake and quickly said, "My apologies, my apologies. Infinite Buddha of the Dao."

"That's 'Infinite Blessings of the Dao.'* If you want Buddha's hand, buy it yourself. Hey, young man, do you believe in anything?"

"Yes, I follow the Flying Spaghetti Monster."

"What the hell is that?"

"Flying Spaghetti. It means the world was created by a true god. That god looks like a bowl of noodles. To foreigners, it's spaghetti. Here, it's plain noodles, zhajiang noodles, big bowl wide noodles..."

The driver was dumbfounded, air conditioning blasting, and said, "Are you hungry? I still have some egg rolls... Hey, why is there a kid among those monks?"

He stared out the window in surprise. Luo Binhan followed his gaze and saw a youth squeezing out from the row of gray-robed monks. The youth's clothes were also strange—a semi-antique red robe, but his hair was a mess of spiky short hair.

Catching a glimpse of that familiar back, Luo Binhan almost jumped out of his seat.

"Mister, stop!"

The bewildered driver pulled over. Luo Binhan paid as fast as he could, then grabbed his suitcase and rushed out.

He ran to the cathedral entrance. By then, the red-robed youth had vanished. The lined-up monks looked at him in puzzlement. Their shiny bald heads triggered very bad memories for Luo Binhan.

"There... there shouldn't be anyone inside, right?"

Luo Binhan swallowed, scanning each monk's face. Fortunately, none of them were anyone he knew.

Just then, the cathedral doors opened. Black-robed nuns filed out, inviting the monks inside. Luo Binhan's eyes lit up again—he noticed that the few nuns in the front were particularly beautiful. The black robes wrapped their bodies tightly, yet their faces were so beautiful they were hard to look away from.

The monks went in. Then a beautiful nun came up to Luo Binhan and said, "Would this gentleman like to come in?"

Luo Binhan was stunned for a moment, then said, "Is that appropriate?"

The nun looked at him tenderly. Her eyes were especially beautiful, clear as crystal.

"The Lord does not reject any lost soul."

Luo Binhan, follower of the Big Bowl Wide Noodles, wanted to sternly reject the Lord, but the Lord's nun was simply too beautiful. He felt his head spinning, and his field of vision was consumed by those autumn-water eyes.

"Please follow me."

He followed the nun. They didn't enter the main hall at all, but went to a small side room. Such unusual behavior didn't register with Luo Binhan; his mind was entirely filled with the nun's murmured whispers.

"Please sit here."

Luo Binhan sat down in the armchair. It was the only chair in the entire side room. Somehow, it came with mismatched rusty handcuffs and foot shackles. The nun tenderly and considerately locked them on him, very tightly.

Luo Binhan began to feel something was off. Why would a leather chair have iron handcuffs?

The nun pressed her hands together in front of her chest, walked gracefully to him, and gave a gentle smile. Suddenly, Luo Binhan's mind cleared.

He looked at his bound hands and feet and started screaming in terror. When the nun began to undress, he howled even more desperately.

"Let's talk this out! Don't use your looks to commit crimes!"

The nun shed the outer black, heavy fabric. Underneath, her body was curvaceous, tempting beyond words. But over her jade-like skin was something else—a strange tight outfit made of thin chains, leather, and metal plates. A light armor that only protected the vital areas.

She reached behind her back. Luo Binhan thought her waist was so slender it could be encircled by a single hand, that beautiful body tightly wrapped in the tight outfit. Yet from somewhere she produced something like a stun gun. She pressed it against Luo Binhan's throat with a click and asked, "Where is the Degradation Hound?"

"Is this him?" asked a nun with a prominent chest.

"He's in the 0206 records," replied another whose waist was particularly sparing of fabric.

The nuns all fixed their star-like beautiful eyes on him. "Miya, you should be watching outside," said the palest-skinned nun.

The nun called Miya didn't take off her clothes. She collapsed without a word, revealing a young man in a red robe behind her.

"You're too noisy," the young man said contemptuously to Luo Binhan.

That voice ignited Luo Binhan's hope. He recognized the owner of the voice—his name was Jing Huang, a strange young man he had met by chance a few months ago. The young man had many unsolved mysteries, but none of that mattered now.

Jing Huang kicked the fallen nun and walked slowly into the hall. With more mechanical clicks, the nuns turned in unison to face him. Luo Binhan was tied up tightly and couldn't see what they were pulling out, but frankly, he didn't really want to know.

"Shoot," the young man said expressionlessly.

"Aim carefully," added Luo Binhan, who could only sit straight in front of the nuns.

The nuns opened fire. Luo Binhan thought that hearing explosions so close might deafen him, but the sound from behind him wasn't a loud boom; it was a very short chirping sound. He almost thought it was birdsong.

Luo Binhan saw countless thin red rays shoot from behind him. They flew past his scalp toward the side hall door, weaving into a bright red grid that sizzled on the floor. Scared, he instinctively closed his eyes.

So he couldn't say much about what happened next. He felt a scorching wind blow from behind him. The gale raged through the entire hall, sending the furnishings flying everywhere. Luo Binhan sensed something solid moving around him—calling it moving because it was very agile, stopping and flying around him like a fish swimming in the air.

He dared to open his eyes. A series of black shadows shot past his face.

The side hall was unrecognizable, on fire everywhere. The nuns lay on the floor, hands and feet covered in blood. Luo Binhan almost thought they were dead, but when a flame licked one of their fingers, she convulsed in pain.

The red-robed young man stood in the center of the hall, stepping on the palest-skinned nun. He kicked her, forcing her to look at him.

"...Xuanhong's Jade," the nun said.

The young man kicked her again: "Who told you to speak? What do you want with 0312?"

His kick wasn't heavy, but the nun writhed in pain. Jing Huang coldly grabbed her hair and pressed his right index finger to her forehead.

The nun began to scream as if electrocuted. Her eyes rolled back, and her creamy skin emerged with patches of burnt black spots.

Jing Huang clicked his tongue, withdrew his hand, and threw her back to the ground. Then he walked toward Luo Binhan. Only then did Luo Binhan realize that the shackles binding him had been cut into several pieces. He quickly jumped up from the chair.

"Hey, Jing Huang, these women—"

"Not dead, just broken limbs. They'll be fine after lying down for a while."

The young man pushed him expressionlessly out of the hall. Although Luo Binhan was eager to leave, he couldn't help looking back.

"Where have you been for the past three months..."

"City of Gates."

"Huh?"

"You wouldn't know even if I told you. I was planning to go farther, but halfway I heard those people were coming. The arrays you set up at home couldn't stop them, so I decided to take you away first."

Luo Binhan was a bit dazed, so he changed the subject: "Were those women looking for you?"

"No, the ones looking for me haven't arrived yet. They came to kill 0312."

The 0312 Jing Huang mentioned was another strange man Luo Binhan had met a few months ago. The man claimed his surname was Fu Ke and his given name Ke, working as a programmer in a tech company in the neighboring city. His most striking feature was a bald head as glaring as a monk's.

Like Jing Huang, Fu Ke had disappeared three months ago, right around the time Luo Binhan's friend Zhou Yu was discharged from the hospital. But at the time, Luo Binhan wasn't surprised; he knew both Jing Huang and Fu Ke had secrets different from ordinary people.

That was—

"Get in the car," Jing Huang said.

Luo Binhan stared at the flashy red sports car in front of him. The joy of reunion vanished instantly.

"Jing Huang!" he roared. "You took my fucking car again!"

"This is my car."

"Bullshit! You don't even have a driver's license!"

The young man frowned impatiently: "What are you shouting about? I said it's mine, so it's mine. No time to explain. Get in the car!"

He kicked Luo Binhan and his suitcase into the back seat, then moved to the driver's side and started the engine. Luo Binhan gasped as the suitcase hit him, barely managing to sit up. He was about to argue when he saw the scenery outside the car and forgot everything in fear.

The sports car was speeding down the city streets at an estimated speed of over eighty kilometers per hour. But strangely, the engine sounded silent.

Eighty kilometers wasn't an exact estimate, and the dashboard seemed broken, motionless. But based on his experience driving on highways, Luo Binhan knew the actual speed was only higher. He almost screamed again—compared to the laser guns of those pervert lingerie nuns, hitting anything at this speed in the city would be a more miserable death. And perhaps dying instantly would be better than surviving the crash.

"Slow the fuck dow—huh?"

The car jolted slightly, the front wheels tilting upward noticeably. Was there a ramp on this city road? Before Luo Binhan could figure it out, the familiar feeling of weightlessness enveloped him. Right before his eyes, the car's rear wheels left the ground, and the whole car flew into the air like a glider.

To be honest, this wasn't Luo Binhan's first time experiencing something like this. Silently, he stared up at the blue sky for a moment, then pulled out his phone and called Zhou Yu.

"I'm ascending to heaven," he said calmly. "Remember to feed my parrot."

On the other end of the line, Zhou Yu said, "Huh?"

Luo Binhan wanted to ask more, but at that moment the carriage began to dissolve—he couldn't find a better word to describe it. The entire outer shell of the car wriggled and reorganized, soft as chocolate in high heat, eventually becoming a completely sealed cylindrical space.

Jing Huang raised his head, aimed at a black ring above—originally a gourd car ornament on Luo Binhan's sports car.

"Aralega, I'm bringing someone up. Take over the sub-pod."

A few seconds after the boy finished speaking, Luo Binhan suddenly felt a strong sense of gravitational disorientation. Although the car's windows had already disappeared in the previous dissolution, he immediately realized they were ascending. How high? He had no feeling after the acceleration stopped. Was this really his car? Were they ascending on four-cylinder gasoline?

He couldn't stop his mind from wandering, but honestly, he wasn't that panicked. That was thanks to a strange incident he'd experienced a few months ago.

It sounded like a weird story from a street stall, so he couldn't even tell his family. But it was indeed Luo Binhan's personal experience—he'd been kidnapped by two giant flies driving a UFO, and nearly left his planet forever. He still hadn't figured out the ins and outs of that incident, but it was because of that encounter that he'd met the boy called Jing Huang, who had temporarily lived in his home for a while.

The experiences during that time were frankly unpleasant, but when Jing Huang suddenly disappeared, Luo Binhan couldn't help feeling a little regret. However, at the time he was more focused on his hospitalized friend Zhou Yu, so he didn't bother much about Jing Huang's whereabouts.

A slight feeling of weightlessness made him feel itchy inside. The rising cylindrical container seemed to be decelerating. Luo Binhan started to feel nauseous. He stole a glance at Jing Huang in front and found the boy seemed to be dozing.

—As for Jing Huang, Luo Binhan couldn't claim to know him deeply. During the time he housed the boy, his favorite activity was slouching on the sofa watching TV, all shows Luo Binhan had no idea why they existed—Korean dramas, Qing dynasty palace dramas, idol dramas...

From morning till night, Jing Huang watched these programs with a blank expression. Luo Binhan had no idea what amusement the boy got from them; he always felt that the look in his eyes was like he was watching a nature documentary. And when Luo Binhan tried to recommend his favorite sci-fi movies, the boy would always stare at the ceiling in boredom.

What was even harder to handle was Jing Huang's destructive power over appliances. Just in the first week they knew each other, Luo Binhan lost his kitchen light, refrigerator, and microwave, and he still didn't know how the boy had broken those things.

Luo Binhan was born into a large family. In this complex Luo clan, his father was both the most authoritative eldest son and commanded enviable wealth. Luo Binhan—despite having many nominal and illegitimate younger siblings—was his father's legitimate eldest son and publicly designated heir to the estate. This brought quite complicated influences to his life, some good and some bad, but one thing was undoubtedly enviable—he never lacked money.

So he replaced the kitchen light, refrigerator, and microwave in his solo apartment, and bought an extra small TV for his bedroom. This gave him a certain degree of peace during his cohabitation with Jing Huang.

But did Jing Huang's weirdness end there? No, no, far from it.

In the few short months Luo Binhan spent with him, he saw Jing Huang do at least three extraordinary things: Jing Huang once used a green flame to burn someone who looked exactly like him to ashes; Jing Huang could summon typhoon-like violent air currents indoors; Jing Huang could play tunes on a leaf to command birds and beasts.

When the boy first came to Luo Binhan's home, his way of speaking was also very strange. He could understand Luo Binhan and answer in the same language, but aside from unusually fluent profanity, normal conversation often carried inexplicably archaic tones. He could write traditional Chinese characters with a brush, though the characters often had stroke errors, and he could predict the next day's weather based on the clouds and stars.

Suppose it were an outsider, Luo Binhan thought more than once: suppose a person familiar with modern pop culture were suddenly thrown into his home and saw all of Jing Huang's oddities, they would naturally come to one conclusion: Jing Huang was a Daoist cultivator who had come down from the deep mountains.

But that was an illusion. Jing Huang didn't come from any deep mountain or old forest; he came from beyond the sky, beyond the stars, beyond the distance between sun and moon. How far that was, Luo Binhan had no idea.

The vehicle vibrated slightly, as if moving through a tunnel of just the right size, then clicked into some slot.

The top cover opened, and strong light poured in from outside. Jing Huang stood up first and jumped out easily through the opening, while Luo Binhan climbed out with some difficulty, clinging to the edge.

The space outside was so bright it dazzled him, and he couldn't immediately see his surroundings. He heard Jing Huang's voice shouting angrily.

"Ju xie qi? He yi! Zhi xie!"

Luo Binhan couldn't understand what the boy was saying, but he wasn't unfamiliar with the language, because its unique and complex tonal fluctuations sounded at first like singing in some particularly obscure dialect.

"Haha, sorry, sorry, please forgive me, I was just too happy earlier..."

The second voice that followed was a young and cheerful male voice. Luo Binhan understood his words without any difficulty. As soon as the young man finished, the surrounding bright light dimmed.

Luo Binhan opened his eyes.

He stood in a narrow rectangular hall. There were several people—or at least humanoid beings—standing in the hall. Jing Huang was furiously grabbing a young man by the hair and cursing, forcing the taller man to bend down and apologize politely.

The scene was bizarre, but Luo Binhan didn't have time to dwell on it. His gaze was like a small piece of iron attracted by a magnet, firmly stuck on a window nearby.

Its edges were inlaid with cold blue light strips, a window without corners.

Behind the window, the night sky sparkled with stars. In the center of that sparse and beautiful sea of stars, Luo Binhan saw a huge azure planet. It floated silently in his view, slowly rotating. Through the light white clouds, Luo Binhan could even make out his own country and continent—but that was all before today. What would happen from now on, he couldn't answer at all at this moment.

The secret of the boy named Jing Huang, what made him different from ordinary people, lay here. In the past, Luo Binhan had encountered cults that sacrificed humans, ancient books recording mysterious rituals, and strange dogs that smiled at people. But none could compare to Jing Huang's secret.

He was an alien.

Chapter 4: Pan-Sapiens Anthropocentrism (Part 1)

Luo Binhan was a man with considerable experience traveling abroad.

In his suitcase prepared for his planned trip abroad, he had packed three sets of a change of clothes, two pairs of socks, a toiletry kit, several boxes of specialty snacks from Pear Sea City, a phone charger and plug adapter, exchanged cash, a passport, a water bottle, and a pack of napkins. Additionally, in his pockets were his phone, wallet, and a lighter. The wallet contained credit cards, change, a keychain, ID documents, and a few one-inch photos.

About the lighter: Luo Binhan sometimes—such as when he learned that his unlucky little sister had caused a string of troubles—had a habit of smoking, but not a strong addiction. Carrying a lighter was more of a hobby of his. It was a rather extravagant silver lighter, finely engraved with arabesque patterns. On the day Luo Binhan came of age, Zhou Yu had given it to him as a coming-of-age gift, back when Zhou Yu's childhood sweetheart still hadn't disappeared.

Thinking about this, Luo Binhan felt a bit baffled. He didn't intend to place blame on Zhou Yu, but he nonetheless felt that his friend's luck was rather poor. Zhou Yu had lost his childhood sweetheart—who could well be called his child bride—a year ago, and now his best friend was about to be taken away by a UFO descending from the sky. What an unfortunate thing that would be for that reclusive medical student. Was this the end? Would Zhou Yu have to spend his life accompanied by a parrot?

But now was not the time to consider Zhou Yu and the parrot.

Luo Binhan and all his personal belongings were brought to a circular hall. It looked like a fairly normal rest area, with soft chairs, bookshelves, and a television (at least it looked like one), and there were even several glowing potted plants on the table. The entire circular hall was brightly lit, but the light source was untraceable. Embedded in the very center of the hall's floor was a large sheet of transparent glass, beneath which stretched the deep, boundless void of space. It seemed to be a cosmic version of a glass skybridge.

Luo Binhan cautiously skirted around that area with a touch of reverence. From the stars streaking backward, he judged that the spacecraft was moving at high speed. Where was the sun? He had no memory of when the ship had passed that massive fireball.

Just then, a green-haired person drifted over. He said to Luo Binhan: "You know the floor is a simulated image, right?"

"Huh?"

"Arrangement, arrangement. Some statistics claim that the placement of living matter contains intrinsic order, and it often reveals certain unconscious traits of the population living here. My name is '∈'—ah, in your culture, this mathematical symbol is quite common, you know? Do you know that there are linguistic divisions on your planet? He continued deeper into the round hall, went down the stairs, and passed through a corridor. This path looked more and more familiar to Luo Binhan.

"Huh?" he said. "What do you want to do?"

Jing Huang stopped in front of Luo Binhan's room. He was silent for a moment, then kicked the door open. Luo Binhan saw another version of himself lying on the bed, sprawled out, snoring loudly. He suddenly had a bad feeling.

Then Jing Huang raised his foot again. He kicked the entire bed over.

Luo Binhan suddenly felt the world spinning. Some force squeezed him, compressing him into an infinitely small point. He resisted desperately, then painfully opened his eyes. He found himself crushed under the overturned bed, nearly suffocated by the quilt and mattress.

"You were having a nightmare," Jing Huang said when he crawled out.

Luo Binhan wiped his face: "You'll repeat the year sooner or later."

Jing Huang ignored him. He kept circling the room, as if searching for a lurking ghost.

"What are you doing?"

"That dragon is playing tricks," Jing Huang said. A faint glimmer lit up around him.

Luo Binhan leaned against the wall, dazedly recalling the dream he just had. He said to Jing Huang, "I dreamed of that dragon. She told me you are the child of destiny, the envoy of light, the savior of the universe, the light novel protagonist—in short, you'll be awesome in the future."

"Listen to it bullshit," Jing Huang said. "Old dragons are all fucking professional liars. The last guy who was fooled by a dragon is still lying in his grave."

Luo Binhan couldn't help feeling a bit disappointed. In the dream, he had wanted to get rid of the dragon, but after waking up, he found what he saw in the dream strangely interesting. He really liked that inexplicable floating sensation.

At that moment, the whole room began to shake.

Luo Binhan was already somewhat used to it. He ran to the deck with Jing Huang and found a tsunami behind The Silent.

Flames surged high, obscuring half the sky. Compared to it, The Silent was like a leaf in a rainstorm. In the blink of an eye, the towering wall of fire had already reached the stern.

Jing Huang flew up. He pointed at the ship and shouted urgently. A sphere made of translucent film wrapped around The Silent.

The fiery wave poured down, engulfing the entire sphere in flames.

In that instant, Luo Binhan felt he seemed to see something. It was hidden deep beneath the ocean of flames, huge, cold, bright, like a mountain cast from white iron. Due to its unimaginable size, Luo Binhan's limited vision couldn't even discern its specific shape.

His eyeballs began to burn painfully, as if they had turned into two fireballs, burning through flesh and boiling his brain. The pain was indescribable, yet for some reason he couldn't look away, only staring helplessly at that silvery-white thing. He felt himself evaporating from the marrow, about to turn into carefree dust in the radiance.

Jing Huang descended from the sky and kicked him to the ground.

"If you don't want to die, don't look," he pressed Luo Binhan's head down tightly. "This entire stellar layer is made of that old thing's dream, and you still want to find its true form? Do you believe that those things up there will blow you away in three seconds?"

Luo Binhan gasped desperately. His brain was boiling hot, his thoughts fragmented, and he only wanted to scream hysterically. Not until Jing Huang helped him into the cabin did sensation gradually return to his body.

Jing Huang called Momoro and told him to take Luo Binhan to get some water, while he himself started doing stretching exercises on the spot.

Momoro asked, "Mr. Xuanhong, what are you doing?"

"I'm going out to fight," Jing Huang said expressionlessly. "I'll strip a dragon's sinew for that old bastard at home."

Luo Binhan raised his hand weakly: "Can you beat it, young master? Don't flood Chentang Pass."

"Flooding would be best."

Jing Huang swung his arms and walked toward the cabin exit, when a silvery-white jellyfish floated in from outside.

"I apologize for the accident just now," it said in an Aethereal voice. "It seems that wasn't a good time to turn over."

"You fucking did it on purpose, didn't you?"

The jellyfish spun in the air, its row of transparent tentacles twirling like a dancer's skirt.

"I intend to help you, Xuanhong," it said. "Since you have accepted the punishment I proposed and resolutely refuse to return to your homeland, I plan to offer assistance in another way—if your ship drifts with the current, it will reach the edge of my dream in one month. However, if I turn over gently, in the gentlest manner, the tides will send you out of this region at the fastest speed. Before the end of tomorrow, you will arrive at the City of a Thousand Gates and Ten Thousand Doors."

"...Can't you use some other method to speed up? That snake guy even fucking knows how to build a subway!"

"The Lord of Rain City is an exception among us," the jellyfish replied. "He is very good at imitating your tiny and delicate devices, but for a creature like me, they are a bit redundant. I can turn a few times to send you off, or you can continue sailing on your own. I must warn you that danger is approaching; there are many voices in the tides inquiring about your whereabouts. If you don't race against time, your path ahead will be fraught with countless difficulties."

Jing Huang was silent, then finally asked, "You won't smash the ship, will you?"

"I think your protection is safe enough," the jellyfish replied, "but it might cause a slight dizziness."

Jing Huang acquiesced. The jellyfish began to roll leisurely in the air, then suddenly said to Luo Binhan, "I apologize for the accident just now, and I have placed a gift in your pocket."

Luo Binhan was stunned. He reached into his pocket and first felt the lighter, then the marble, and finally a larger, round, rough-surfaced sphere.

A hickory nut. Its shell emitted a rich fruity aroma.

"I believe this can somewhat compensate for the trouble I caused," the jellyfish said. "Farewell, Child of Twin Stars. When the Tenth Month rises, I hope to hear your name again."

It floated toward the cabin exit. Jing Huang chased after it, and Luo Binhan also wanted to go, but Momoro stopped him, saying, "Mr. Luo, you'd better rest a bit."

Luo Binhan had no mind to rest. The fragrance of the hickory nut made his heart pound. With Momoro's help, he returned to the deck.

The jellyfish disappeared into the flames outside the sphere. Then the boundless sea of fire began to change. First it decomposed into countless thin strips, and finally twisted into a huge fire column reaching to the sky.

The fire column lashed out like a whip.

It struck heavily against the bubble surrounding The Silent, and the entire ship flew off like a golf ball hit—this was the last scene Luo Binhan saw before fainting.
