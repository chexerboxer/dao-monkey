Chapter 3: Pear Sea City Religious and Cultural Exchange Festival (Part 3)

The call cut off abruptly.

Luo Binhan put down his phone in despair and looked down at the ground from the car window. He often took flights abroad to visit his mother, so he was quite sensitive to altitude; at the moment, he was at an estimated altitude of about eight hundred meters. That made him shiver a little from the wind, but he wasn't completely chilled.

He kicked the driver's seat in front of him.

"Hey, Jing Huang, where the fuck are you taking me?"

Jing Huang, sitting in the driver's seat, turned his head. Luo Binhan noticed that his hands weren't even on the steering wheel, but that no longer scared him.

"First, we'll meet up with Aralega and the others, then go to the City of Gates," Jing Huang said. "We'll drop you back when the heat's off."

"So where the hell is the City of Gates?"

Jing Huang frowned in slight displeasure: "On a neutral stellar layer near the Farless domain, on the border of the Confederation."

"So where the fuck is that? Andromeda? Sagittarius?"

"I told you, it's not on your stellar layer."

Luo Binhan didn't quite understand, but he summarized in his own way: "Another world?"

"...Just think of it that way."

"Do people there have superpowers?" Luo Binhan asked suddenly, on a whim.

"...Depends."

"Don't you like playing with fire? Can everyone there do that?"

"...Do you eat grass?"

"Huh?"

Jing Huang said coldly, "You don't eat grass, so why do you live on the same planet as horses?"

Luo Binhan wanted to ask more, but at that moment the carriage began to dissolve—he couldn't find a better word to describe it. The entire outer shell of the car wriggled and reorganized, soft as chocolate in high heat, eventually becoming a completely sealed cylindrical space.

Jing Huang raised his head, aimed at a black ring above—originally a gourd car ornament on Luo Binhan's sports car.

"Aralega, I'm bringing someone up. Take over the sub-pod."

A few seconds after the boy finished speaking, Luo Binhan suddenly felt a strong sense of gravitational disorientation. Although the car's windows had already disappeared in the previous dissolution, he immediately realized they were ascending. How high? He had no feeling after the acceleration stopped. Was this really his car? Were they ascending on four-cylinder gasoline?

He couldn't stop his mind from wandering, but honestly, he wasn't that panicked. That was thanks to a strange incident he'd experienced a few months ago.

It sounded like a weird story from a street stall, so he couldn't even tell his family. But it was indeed Luo Binhan's personal experience—he'd been kidnapped by two giant flies driving a UFO, and nearly left his planet forever. He still hadn't figured out the ins and outs of that incident, but it was because of that encounter that he'd met the boy called Jing Huang, who had temporarily lived in his home for a while.

The experiences during that time were frankly unpleasant, but when Jing Huang suddenly disappeared, Luo Binhan couldn't help feeling a little regret. However, at the time he was more focused on his hospitalized friend Zhou Yu, so he didn't bother much about Jing Huang's whereabouts.

A slight feeling of weightlessness made him feel itchy inside. The rising cylindrical container seemed to be decelerating. Luo Binhan started to feel nauseous. He stole a glance at Jing Huang in front and found the boy seemed to be dozing.

—As for Jing Huang, Luo Binhan couldn't claim to know him deeply. During the time he housed the boy, his favorite activity was slouching on the sofa watching TV, all shows Luo Binhan had no idea why they existed—Korean dramas, Qing dynasty palace dramas, idol dramas...

From morning till night, Jing Huang watched these programs with a blank expression. Luo Binhan had no idea what amusement the boy got from them; he always felt that the look in his eyes was like he was watching a nature documentary. And when Luo Binhan tried to recommend his favorite sci-fi movies, the boy would always stare at the ceiling in boredom.

What was even harder to handle was Jing Huang's destructive power over appliances. Just in the first week they knew each other, Luo Binhan lost his kitchen light, refrigerator, and microwave, and he still didn't know how the boy had broken those things.

Luo Binhan was born into a large family. In this complex Luo clan, his father was both the most authoritative eldest son and commanded enviable wealth. Luo Binhan—despite having many nominal and illegitimate younger siblings—was his father's legitimate eldest son and publicly designated heir to the estate. This brought quite complicated influences to his life, some good and some bad, but one thing was undoubtedly enviable—he never lacked money.

So he replaced the kitchen light, refrigerator, and microwave in his solo apartment, and bought an extra small TV for his bedroom. This gave him a certain degree of peace during his cohabitation with Jing Huang.

But did Jing Huang's weirdness end there? No, no, far from it.

In the few short months Luo Binhan spent with him, he saw Jing Huang do at least three extraordinary things: Jing Huang once used a green flame to burn someone who looked exactly like him to ashes; Jing Huang could summon typhoon-like violent air currents indoors; Jing Huang could play tunes on a leaf to command birds and beasts.

When the boy first came to Luo Binhan's home, his way of speaking was also very strange. He could understand Luo Binhan and answer in the same language, but aside from unusually fluent profanity, normal conversation often carried inexplicably archaic tones. He could write traditional Chinese characters with a brush, though the characters often had stroke errors, and he could predict the next day's weather based on the clouds and stars.

Suppose it were an outsider, Luo Binhan thought more than once: suppose a person familiar with modern pop culture were suddenly thrown into his home and saw all of Jing Huang's oddities, they would naturally come to one conclusion: Jing Huang was a Daoist cultivator who had come down from the deep mountains.

But that was an illusion. Jing Huang didn't come from any deep mountain or old forest; he came from beyond the sky, beyond the stars, beyond the distance between sun and moon. How far that was, Luo Binhan had no idea.

The vehicle vibrated slightly, as if moving through a tunnel of just the right size, then clicked into some slot.

The top cover opened, and strong light poured in from outside. Jing Huang stood up first and jumped out easily through the opening, while Luo Binhan climbed out with some difficulty, clinging to the edge.

The space outside was so bright it dazzled him, and he couldn't immediately see his surroundings. He heard Jing Huang's voice shouting angrily.

"Ju xie qi? He yi! Zhi xie!"

Luo Binhan couldn't understand what the boy was saying, but he wasn't unfamiliar with the language, because its unique and complex tonal fluctuations sounded at first like singing in some particularly obscure dialect.

"Haha, sorry, sorry, please forgive me, I was just too happy earlier..."

The second voice that followed was a young and cheerful male voice. Luo Binhan understood his words without any difficulty. As soon as the young man finished, the surrounding bright light dimmed.

Luo Binhan opened his eyes.

He stood in a narrow rectangular hall. There were several people—or at least humanoid beings—standing in the hall. Jing Huang was furiously grabbing a young man by the hair and cursing, forcing the taller man to bend down and apologize politely.

The scene was bizarre, but Luo Binhan didn't have time to dwell on it. His gaze was like a small piece of iron attracted by a magnet, firmly stuck on a window nearby.

Its edges were inlaid with cold blue light strips, a window without corners.

Behind the window, the night sky sparkled with stars. In the center of that sparse and beautiful sea of stars, Luo Binhan saw a huge azure planet. It floated silently in his view, slowly rotating. Through the light white clouds, Luo Binhan could even make out his own country and continent—but that was all before today. What would happen from now on, he couldn't answer at all at this moment.

The secret of the boy named Jing Huang, what made him different from ordinary people, lay here. In the past, Luo Binhan had encountered cults that sacrificed humans, ancient books recording mysterious rituals, and strange dogs that smiled at people. But none could compare to Jing Huang's secret.

He was an alien.
